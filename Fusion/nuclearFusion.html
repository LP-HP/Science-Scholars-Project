    • Nuclear Fusion

    Introduction

    Nuclear fusion is the opposite of fusion. Instead of an atom splitting, it is when two nuclei "fuse" together, hence the name. It is very powerful and releases more energy than fission.

    Nuclear fusion in thermonuclear weapons

    After the fission bomb proved devastating, scientists around the world tried to produce a more deadly weapon using fusion. The United States were the first to test a thermonuclear weapon in Operation Ivy. Thermonuclear bombs have 2 stages: First an implosion happens, powered by plutonium/uranium and the inside is filled with hydrogen fuel (normally tritium, an isotope of hydrogen). Second is the more powerful fusion. The fission explosion releases lots of gamma rays and x-rays, which are channeled down into the fusion compartment. Foam surrounds the fuel, normally lithium deuteride (formed by fusing deuterium and lithium). The foam melts into plasma, which causes a “sparkplug”, a casing of the fuel, to release explosive energy. The tritium then fuses with the deuterium (fusion) to form helium and more neutrons. These neutrons go and cause more fission reactions, further pressuring the fuel, causing even more fusion reactions. This is thermonuclear fusion, different from normal fusion because it turns into plasma and the high temperature makes particles fuse with each other.

    Uses of nuclear fusion

    Humans have not really harnessed the power of nuclear fusion yet, but stars are powered by this force. Our sun runs on nuclear fusion. Humans have only used fusion for thermonuclear weapons, but efforts are underway to use fusion for power plants.

    Dangers

    Nuclear fusion, despite being promising for energy production, poses a threat to humanity. With thermonuclear weapons around the world, all it takes is a couple for a massive war to start. This war will be on a scale never seen before, utilizing thermonuclear weapons to turn the world into a wasteland. Nuclear fusion also has a lot of negative enviormental impacts, as it can leave behind radioactive residue such as uranium and other things that could be potentially harmful to the world. Like with all things, fusion must be handled with caution if we want a bright future.

    Driving Forces

    There are many forces at work when nuclear fusion occurs, but the most prominent one is the nuclear force. Both the strong and weak force play a major part in fusion. When the particles get close enough, the attractive nuclear force will outweigh the repelling force and allows the two nuclei to fuse.

    Extra

    Operation Ivy, the first thermonuclear test, consisted of two thermonuclear bomb detonations. They were detonated in Enewetak Atoll in the Pacific Proving Ground in the Marshall Islands.

    The Sun will run out of fuel and die in approximately 10 billion years.

    Nuclear fusion releases about four million times more energy than coal and four times more energy that fission, assuming they are of equal masses.
